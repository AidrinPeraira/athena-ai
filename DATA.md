# Introduction and some tips

- HR Interviews are never just about answering questions. It is also about connecting with the interviewer and building a rapport.
- It is supposed to be a conversation. You can ask questions to the interviewer as well.
- Make sure that you are not just answering questions but also making a connection with the interviewer.
- Try to leave a good impression of yourself.
- Present yourself well. Neat. Clean. Professional.
- Don't jump to answers. Take a moment to think about the question and structure your answer if you need it. A pause is better than a wrong answer.
- Always refresh your memory of the job description and the company before the interview.
- Get a brief idea about the people who are going to interview you.
- Learn about things like
  - What is the company doing?
  - Who runs this?
  - Which market or industry is this company focusing on?
  - How can you help?
  - What is the company's mission and vision?

# Importance of Story Telling in Interviews

- Why does story telling matter? Because it give the listener the following
  - Reliability: It makes your answer more believable. A story acts more like a proof of what you are saying.
  - Impact: A well structured story can give the listener the right idea about your skills and personality. It can help you portray your idea to the listener without ambiguity.
  - Memory: Stories are easier to remember than facts and figures. Also people tend to connect with stories more than facts and figures.
- There is always the choice, to give a direct answer or narrate an answer as a story. Choose the latter when needed
- Use it to answer situational & behavioural questions
- Use frameworks (give structure)
  - STAR: Situation Task Action Result
  - CAR: Challenge Action Result
  - …etc

# CAR method to structure answers

## C- Challenge

- Give a brief context (no more than 2-3 lines).
- What was the project/scenario? Who was involved?
- Why was this task important?

## A- Action

- What specific steps did you take (individually or as a team)?
- What tools, technologies, or methods did you use?
- What was your decision-making process?
- Be specific and use "I" statements.

## R- Result

- What was the outcome of your actions?
- Quantify the result whenever possible (e.g., "improved performance by X%", "reduced errors by Y", "saved Z hours").
- What did you learn from the experience?
- How did this outcome impact the project or organization?

# STAR method to structure answers

## S- Situation

- Give a brief context (no more than 2-3 lines).
- What was the project/scenario? Who was involved?
- Why was this task important?

## T- Task

- What was your specific responsibility?
- What was the objective or goal you had to achieve?
- What constraints or challenges did you face?

## A- Action

- What specific steps did you take (individually or as a team)?
- What tools, technologies, or methods did you use?
- What was your decision-making process?
- Be specific and use "I" statements.

## R- Result

- What was the outcome of your actions?
- Quantify the result whenever possible (e.g., "improved performance by X%", "reduced errors by Y", "saved Z hours").
- What did you learn from the experience?
- How did this outcome impact the project or organization?

# Types of questions you might encounter during the interview

## Behavioral questions

- A type of question that asks about your past experiences and behaviors. It is used to assess your skills, abilities, and personality.
- Use past experience to predict future behavior
- While answering these questions, structure your answer in a way that the interviewer gets a clear picture that you can handle such situations and have the necessary skills and abilities.
- Especially make sure that the interviewer gets a picture that you can trust.

## Situational questions

- A type of question that asks about hypothetical scenarios and how you would handle them. It is used to assess your problem-solving skills, decision-making skills, and conflict resolution skills.
- These questions might come with the option to choose between multiple scenarios.
- Never rush into an answer. Pick a scenario that you are confident you can handle and structure your answer based on it.
- You can always ask questions for clarification or more details if needed to make sure you understand the question properly and help you decide the right answer.
- While answering these questions use logic and reasoning to support your answer.
- Also remember this. You are not required to pick either of the options. You are free to choose a third option if you believe it is the best way to handle the situation. Or you can even select a middle ground.

- Clarity
  - Don’t confuse the interviewer
- Control
  - Don’t lose direction while answering.
  - Don't wander off topic.
- Composure
  - Stay calm even if the question is uncomfortable

# Self Introduction

## Information to include in a self introduction

### Basic Information

- **Name:** John Doe
- **Location:** Kochi, Kerala
- **Current Role:** Full Stack Software Developer

### Current Expertise (Software Development)

- I build **full-stack web solutions** focused on solving real-world problems.
- My development philosophy is rooted in **Object-Oriented Programming (OOP)** principles.
- **Key Technologies:**
- **Backend:** Node.js, Express, TypeScript, JavaScript, Docker
- **Frontend:** React, Next.js

### Previous Background

- **Education:** Include details about your school or college. Also include details of any additional certifications that you have.
- **Experience:** Run through the past jobs and scenarios you have had that can give you work experience relevant to the job you are applying for. Sometimes you might not have worked in a company but you can still use other experience on projects you have worked on, internships, freelance work etc.. to show your skills. Don't waste time elaborating about all aspects of your past experience. Focus on what is relevant to the job you are applying for. Keep it brief.

- **Key Professional Skills:**
- Professional Skills include:
  - Effective Communication and Presentation
  - Teamwork and Collaboration
  - Problem-Solving and Critical Thinking
  - Adaptability and Flexibility
  - Time Management and Organization

### Transition to Tech

- If you are switching careers, make sure you mention it here and also mention why you are switching careers. You can also mention how your past experience is relevant to the job you are applying for.
- For example:
  My story is that I discovered coding while looking for tools (spreadsheets, Notion databases) to streamline my engineering business processes. The satisfaction of solving problems and building applications led me to pursue a bootcamp and become a full-stack developer. I am passionate about leveraging my combined experience to build efficient and impactful software solutions.

### Closing Statement

- Don't forget to conclude what you had said. Ask them if they want to know anything else specifically. Is there anything specific about my experience you'd like me to elaborate on?
  I am now actively looking for a role where I can utilize both my technical expertise and my strong professional/project management background to contribute to positive change within a company. Is there anything specific about my experience you'd like me to elaborate on?

### An example for a simple self introduction

"Hello, I'm John Doe, and I'm originally from Kochi, a small but vibrant city in Kerala.

My journey to software development is perhaps unconventional. I hold a B.Tech in **Civil Engineering** and graduated in 2021. Following my graduation, I spent two years gaining hands-on experience as an Onsite Engineer. After earning my engineer’s license, I transitioned into freelancing, which significantly broadened my professional skill set. It was in the pursuit of greater efficiency for my own engineering business that I stumbled upon the power of coding. I started with simple tools—optimizing spreadsheets and setting up database tables in Notion—and eventually found my way to programming. I quickly realized the deep satisfaction that comes from solving small, intricate problems and building functional applications. This passion led me to dedicate myself fully to the field, culminating in a specialized bootcamp.
Now, as a full-stack web developer, I am looking forward to joining a company where I can apply my unique blend of technical skills and real-world project management expertise to drive meaningful change. I'm eager to learn and contribute to a team environment. Is there anything specific about my experience that you'd like me to dive deeper into?"

  "Hello everyone, I’m John Doe. Before I tell you what I build now, let me tell you what I used to build.

  Now, which part of that journey—the site execution or the code stack—would you like to drill into first?"

# What to do when a HR interviewer calls you regarding your application?

- Confirm the name of HR and company
- Verify it is valid HR
- Don’t say you are a fresher
- You are a skilled fresher.
- Don’t ask for an over budget salary package
- If the HR is calling you, and you don't remember the name and details of the company, tell him you are busy and call him back after refreshing the details of the application.
- Don’t give salary expectations. Give a range or ask for a budget and say that seems to be in and around the range.

# More Situational and Behavioural Questions

## Questions based on soft skills.

- Usually two ways this can go

1. What if question?
   - hypothetical question asked by HR
   - Ask for more clarity if needed. Don’t hesitate.
2. Have you ever question?
   - This is based on what the HR thinks we might have experienced
   - If you have one share it
   - If not you can turn it into a hypothetical question. And then answer it as what you would do if you were to face such an issue.

## Questions like the following

- For you what is work life balance and how do you maintain it?
  - Work–life balance isn’t about splitting time 50/50. It’s about being fully present where you are.
  - When at work, focus and give your best. When off, disconnect and recharge so you can come back productive.
  - Mention that balance changes depending on deadlines, but long term it’s about sustainability.
  - You can manage it by staying organized and knowing when to switch off. That helps avoid burnout and stay consistent.

- Would you choose friendship or money?
  - This is a tricky question that tests your values and professionalism.
  - Don't give a simple "money" or "friendship" answer.
  - Frame it around professional context: In a workplace, professional relationships and collaboration (friendship/teamwork) are crucial for long-term success, but fair compensation (money) is also important as a reflection of the value you bring.
  - State that you value a healthy work environment built on good relationships, as that often leads to better productivity and financial success.
  - Keep your answer focussed on the balance between the two and how one is necessary for the other.

- What is the greatest challenge you have faced in ever in your life?
  - Use the STAR or CAR method to structure this answer.
  - Mention a specific challenge you solved.
  - Or emphasize on the lesson learned from a failure. The HR wants to see resilience, problem-solving skills, and growth mindset.
  - Focus on how you overcame it and what you took away from the experience, not just the difficulty of the challenge itself.

- How will you handle the HR telling you that you are no longer needed AI has taken your job?
  - This evaluates your adaptability and attitude towards new technology.
  - Show a growth mindset. State that you view AI as a tool to enhance productivity, not just a replacement.
  - Mention that if a specific role is automated, you would focus on upskilling and adapting to leverage that AI, moving into roles that require human oversight, strategy, or complex problem-solving that AI cannot replicate.
  - Emphasize your continuous learning approach.
  - Tell her that you will always learn and adapt, hence reducing the chances that such a thing might happen.
  - If something like that happens then tell her that you will try to understand what you lacked and ask for constructive feedback on where to improve and what to learn. So you'll be ready for the next oppurtunity.

- What is the one skill you don’t have? how do you plan to acquire it
  - Pick a real, professional skill that is useful but not an absolute dealbreaker for the core requirements of the job.
  - Avoid saying "I have all the skills" or picking a critical flaw.
  - The most important part of the answer is how you plan to acquire it. Show a proactive approach (e.g., "I'm currently taking a course on X," or "I've started reading documentation and building small projects with Y").

- Why should we hire you over someone else with similar technical skills?
  - Focus on your unique blend of soft skills, your background, or your approach to problem-solving.
  - You can mention your ownership, communication skills, or your ability to understand the business side of things, not just the code.
  - Reference your unique background (like a transition from another field) and how it gives you a different perspective on accountability and project lifecycle.

- What will you do if another company ofers you another job with higher package in 6 months from now?
  - Never just say yes. It shows disloyalty.
  - Say that it depends on the circumstances. The salary may be higher but salary is not the only thing that you look for.
  - Emphasize that you value growth, the work environment, and the projects you are working on.
  - You can also say that if you are committed to a project here your first priority will be to ensure that the project is completed successfully. Show that you are a person of integrity.

- What kind of work environment brings out your worst performance?
  - Be careful not to sound too negative or picky.
  - Frame it around an environment that goes against good professional practices.
  - For example, you could say an environment with a lack of clear communication, micromanagement, or where people are siloed and don't collaborate well.
  - Always pivot back to what brings out your best performance (e.g., "I thrive in environments with clear goals and open communication...").

- Have you ever taken ownership of something that wasn’t your responsibility?
  - This is a behavioral question. Use the STAR method.
  - The interviewer is looking for initiative and teamwork.
  - Give an example where you saw a gap or a problem and stepped in to solve it, not for credit, but because it helped the team or the project succeed.

- What motivates you beyond salary?
  - Relate this to the actual work and the company.
  - Mention the satisfaction of solving complex problems, the opportunity to learn and grow, seeing the impact of your work on users, or working with a great team.
  - Show that you are intrinsically motivated by the craft and the results.

- If we hire you today what will be the one thing you will be struggling with in the first three months?
  - This question evaluates self awareness.
  - Pick something realistic but manageable, like getting fully up to speed with specific internal tools or a large existing codebase.
  - Immediately follow up with your mitigation plan: "However, I plan to tackle this by thoroughly reviewing the documentation, asking targeted questions, and taking notes to onboard myself as quickly as possible."
  - You could also mention that intially understanding how the company functions internally will be something that you focus. This is something which is absolutely necessary for proper communication and understanding what to do. Once that is done you can focus on the work.

- Don’t you think the role is too small for your skills?
  - This checks if you might get bored and leave quickly, or if you have an ego.
  - Reframe it positively. Say that you don't view roles as "small."
  - Emphasize that every role offers unique challenges and opportunities to learn. Mention that you are excited to master the fundamentals here, contribute immediately, and look forward to growing with the company and taking on more responsibilities over time.
  - Sample answer: "No, I don't think it's too small. I believe that understanding the fundamentals really well is the key to excelling in any role. I'm excited to master the core responsibilities of this position and contribute effectively to the team from day one. Plus, I see this as a great opportunity to grow with the company and take on more challenges as I develop my skills here."

- Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager what did you do?
  - This checks how you professionally handle things?
  - Give up or fight blindly? No, have a conversation to understand their perspective.
  - Use an example where you presented your case with data or logic, but ultimately respected their final decision if they chose to go a different route, showing that you can disagree and commit.

- We have two candidates better than you. Why should we still hire you?
  - ask why you think they are better than me?
  - What makes you think that they are better?
  - Maybe justify or say you can improve on the feedback.
  - Reiterate your unique strengths and your high level of commitment and adaptability. Keep your composure as it's a stress-test.

- If your team member is not working, but takes credit, what will you do?
  - Show maturity and a focus on the project's success.
  - Don't immediately say you will complain to the manager.
  - Say you would first try to have a private, professional conversation with the team member to understand if they are facing any blockers.
  - Ensure your own contributions are visibly documented (e.g., through commits, meeting notes).
  - If it persists and harms the team, state that you would then escalate it to the manager, focusing on the impact on the work.

- Where do you see yourself in 5 years? Will you still be with us?
  - Focus on professional growth and value addition.
  - Say you see yourself taking on more complex projects, perhaps mentoring others, and deeply understanding the domain.
  - Also say that you lookforward to being in a postion of taking decisions and impacting the project and the company. Also that you will like to be in a position of leadership.
  - Regarding staying: "As long as there are opportunities to learn, be challenged, and contribute meaningfully to the company's goals, I would be very happy to stay and grow my career here."

- If we offer you slightly less than your usual expectation, will you accept?
  - Keep your options open but show that you value the whole package.
  - Say that while salary is important, you are also evaluating the overall opportunity, including the work environment, learning potential, and project quality.
  - Mention that if the role is a perfect fit and offers great growth, you would be open to a discussion to find a mutually agreeable number.
  - Sample answer: "While I have a salary expectation in mind, I'm also evaluating the overall opportunity, including the work environment, learning potential, and project quality. If this role is a great fit and offers significant growth, I would be open to discussing the salary to find a mutually agreeable number."

- You don’t have experience in this tool. Why should we take the risk of hiring you?
  - Highlight your strong fundamentals and track record of learning new things quickly.
  - Give an example of a time you had to learn a completely new tool or technology on the fly and successfully delivered a project.
  - Emphasize that tools change, but problem-solving skills and foundational knowledge remain.

- If selected how soon can you join?
  - Ask them when they expect me to join.
  - Tell them you can try to make it.
  - Else give them the closest one.
  - Tell them if you have any commitments to finish beforehand, which shows professionalism and integrity.

- What do you know about us that is not on our website?
  - This tests how deep your research went.
  - Check their recent news articles, LinkedIn posts from founders/employees, recent product launches, or the specific tech stack they use (if found via a blog or job descriptions).
  - Mentioning something from a recent podcast or interview with their leadership is a great way to stand out.
  - If you know about the industry they are in and how the economics is going to affect that you can also talk about that. And mention that you are interested in their industry and would like to contribute to their success.

## Technical questions

- A type of question that asks about your technical knowledge and skills. It is used to assess your knowledge of programming languages, frameworks, tools, and technologies.
- These kind of questions are rarely asked by HR interviewers. Even if the ask such questions you are not expected to go deep into technical details.
- A simple overview will suffice.

# Critical Questions

- Questions that are designed to test your critical thinking skills and your ability to solve problems. They are often open-ended and require you to think on your feet.

## Critical Questions that are very important to answer correctly

Important questions that If you don’t answer properly. You don’t get the job.

- What will you do if another company offers you a higher position and better pay?
  - This question reflects trust and loyalty.
  - Don’t delay the answer.
  - Answer that I will still take the job you are offering.
  - The reason I applied is because I have interest in working in this company. The culture, the growth. Why would I jump for another company just for pay.

- What could be a reason that you will resign from an organisation?
  - This is to understand your priorities.
  - Not colleagues. Not pay. Not culture
  - If my skills don’t align with company. If it is not valued. Or not respected.

# Cultural fit questions

- A type of question that asks about your personality, values, and work ethic. It is used to assess whether you would be a good fit for the company culture.
- Cultural fit questions are used to evaluate how well you align with the company’s values, beliefs, and work environment.
- These questions are commonly asked by top level mangers like CEOs
- This is your best chance to make a good first impression or kill it. So make the most of it
- Try to make an impression that lasts.

## Cultural Round Questions that you might come across

- From what you know about the company and the work we do here, what is something you'd suggest as an improvement or which aspect will be the first place you'd look for any scope of improvement?
  - This question checks if you know the business and if you are observant.
  - Don't overly criticize the company. Frame your answer as an area for potential growth or an observation based on public information.
  - For example, you could suggest a small UI tweak on their product, an additional feature that competitors have, or an improvement in their public documentation.
  - Alternatively, if you aren't sure, say that you would first need to understand their internal processes before making suggestions, but you would start by looking at user feedback or bottleneck areas.

- What would you do if you are stuck and there is no one to help you?
  - This checks how you handle a crisis. They want to see if you panic or stay calm.
  - Show a structured problem-solving approach.
  - Mention that you would first document the problem and list the options available.
  - Then, you would consult the documentation, search online forums, and try to break the problem into smaller, manageable pieces.
  - If you are still stuck, you would document your attempted solutions and reach out to the team asynchronously (e.g., via Slack or email) so they can help when they are available, while you move on to another unblocked task.

- What does accountability mean to you? Explain a scenario you had to face
  - Use the STAR method to explain a scenario.
  - Define accountability as taking ownership of both the success and the failure of a task, and ensuring that it gets done correctly.
  - Give a scenario where you made a mistake, owned up to it immediately without blaming others, communicated it to the stakeholders, and then took the necessary steps to fix it and prevent it from happening again.

- What is your ideal work environment?
  - Align your answer with what you know about the company's culture.
  - Focus on professional growth, collaboration, and clear communication.
  - You can say you prefer an environment that encourages learning, where team members can openly share ideas, and where there is a strong focus on delivering quality work.
  - Avoid mentioning perks like free food or flexible hours as your main criteria; focus on the work itself.

# Salary Negotiations

- What to do when they start with offering you a low package?
  - Do not immediately reject it, but also do not accept it on the spot.
  - Politely state that based on your market research and the value you bring, you were expecting something in the range of X to Y.
  - Justify your expectation by highlighting your growth: "Over the past few years, I have accomplished X, Y, and Z, and I have acquired [Specific Skills]."
  - Frame it around the value you bring to the role, rather than just what you want.
  - Network with peers in the industry to understand the standard market rate for your skill level so you have a solid benchmark to negotiate from.

- What was your last CTC?
  - Do not hesitate or act uncomfortable.
  - If you prefer not to disclose, you can politely avoid the answer by mentioning that your previous employer has a strict non-disclosure policy regarding compensation.
  - Alternatively, redirect the conversation: "I would prefer to focus on the value I can bring to this role and the budget you have allocated for it. Could you share the range for this position?"
  - Or, you can ask directly: "Will my new offer be based on my current skills and the responsibilities of this role, or strictly on my previous package?"

- We’ll increase your salary after 6 months' probation.
  - Be careful with this promise, as it is often not specific and doesn't guarantee an increase in the 7th month; it may just mean you become _eligible_ for the next cycle.
  - How to handle it: Ask for clarification. Say, "If my performance is satisfactory during the probation period, can I expect the salary increment to reflect exactly from the 7th month?"
  - Ask for this to be documented: "It would be much appreciated if you could add that specific timeline and the Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to the offer letter."
  - If they give a small, namesake increment later:
    - Ask for the reasoning behind the amount.
    - Review the initially agreed-upon KPIs.
    - Ask for constructive feedback on what is required to reach the next compensation tier.
    - Always get agreements written clearly in emails or the official offer.
